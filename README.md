# Zhao_LAB

> Protein Engineering • Synthetic Biology • Biosensors • AI-Driven Biotechnology
> 
The Zhao Lab develops next-generation protein engineering technologies for applications in pharmaceutical science, cell biology, and disease diagnostics.
Our research integrates synthetic biology, microfluidics, machine learning, and high-throughput screening to engineer functional proteins including fluorescent biosensors, enzymes, and antibodies. We aim to accelerate protein design and better understand complex biological systems through large-scale experimental and computational approaches.

## Research Focus

### Biosensor Engineering

We design genetically encoded fluorescent and bioluminescent biosensors capable of monitoring intracellular analytes such as ions and metabolites in live cells with high spatial and temporal resolution.
These biosensors enable researchers to:
- visualize dynamic biochemical events,
- investigate disease mechanisms,
- study cellular metabolism,
- and support therapeutic discovery.
---

### High-Throughput Protein Engineering

Efficient protein design remains a major challenge due to the complexity of sequence–function relationships.
To address this challenge, the Zhao Lab develops high-throughput screening platforms combining:
- microfluidics,
- robotics,
- next-generation sequencing,
- and artificial intelligence.

These systems generate datasets over 1000× larger than traditional manual workflows, enabling large-scale exploration of genotype–phenotype fitness landscapes for proteins such as:
- fluorescent proteins,
- enzymes,
- antibodies,
- and biosensors.

The resulting datasets are used to train machine learning models for efficient and predictive protein engineering.

---

## Impact

The Zhao Lab has developed:
- a microfluidic fluorescence-activated cell sorter (µFACS) with advanced multi-point detection systems,
- genetically encoded fluorescent and bioluminescent biosensors,
- and innovative approaches for studying intracellular metabolism at single-cell resolution.
Notably, the lab pioneered a method for quantifying intracellular citrate levels in mammalian cells using the citrate biosensor **Citron1**.
These biosensors are distributed to researchers worldwide and serve as important tools for studying cell biology, metabolism, and disease mechanisms in both cultured cells and animal models.
